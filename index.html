Albert Camus

A prominent French philosopher, author, and journalist known for his existentialist and absurdist works.

  • 1913: Albert Camus was born on November 7th in Mondovi, French Algeria.
  • 1914-1930: Camus spent his childhood and adolescence in Algiers, Algeria.
  • 1930: Camus moved to mainland France and studied philosophy at the University of Algiers.
  • 1935: Camus contracted tuberculosis, which affected his health for the rest of his life.
  • 1936: Camus joined the French Communist Party briefly but later became disillusioned with communism.
  • 1939: Camus married Simone Hié, a mathematician and philosopher.
  • 1940: With the outbreak of World War II, Camus moved to Paris and became involved in the French Resistance.
  • 1942: Camus published his first novel, "L'Étranger" ("The Stranger"), which gained him widespread recognition.
  • 1943: Camus published his philosophical essay "Le Mythe de Sisyphe" ("The Myth of Sisyphus"), which explored themes of existentialism and the absurd.
  • 1944: Camus joined the French newspaper "Combat" and became its editor during the liberation of Paris.
  • 1947: Camus's play "Caligula" premiered, marking his foray into theater.
  • 1948: Camus's novel "La Peste" ("The Plague") was published, solidifying his literary reputation.
  • 1951: Camus published his novel "L'Homme révolté" ("The Rebel"), which criticized totalitarianism and explored the concept of rebellion.
  • 1957: Camus won the Nobel Prize in Literature for his body of work, particularly citing "L'Étranger" and "La Peste."
  • 1959: Camus died tragically in a car accident on January 4th near Sens, France, at the age of 46.
